US Tariffs on Electronics in 2026: Rates by Product, Country & What to Expect

Updated May 26, 2026 · 8 min read

⚠️ Layered Tariff Alert

Electronics imported from China now face stacked tariffs — base MFN rates + Section 301 duties + IEEPA surcharges. For many product categories, the combined effective rate reaches 30–145%+ depending on the current trade deal status between the US and China.

Electronics is the single largest import category into the United States by value — over $550 billion annually. It's also the most heavily tariffed category from China. If you're importing phones, laptops, televisions, semiconductors, or any consumer electronics product, this guide breaks down exactly what you'll pay in 2026 and where those costs come from.

The tariff picture for electronics has changed dramatically since 2018. Before the trade war, most electronics entered the US duty-free or at very low rates under the WTO's Information Technology Agreement (ITA). Today, if you're sourcing from China, you're dealing with multiple overlapping tariff layers that compound on top of each other.

The Three Tariff Layers on Electronics from China

Understanding your total tariff exposure requires knowing all three layers that apply to Chinese electronics. They don't replace each other — they stack.

1

MFN Base Rate (Most Favored Nation)

The baseline rate that applies to imports from all WTO members. For most electronics — phones, computers, TVs — this rate is 0% under the ITA (Information Technology Agreement). For other electronics like certain cables, connectors, or accessories, MFN rates range from 2.6% to 4.7%. This is the floor, not the ceiling.

2

Section 301 China Tariffs

Applied specifically to Chinese goods since 2018. Electronics fall across multiple "lists" with different rates. List 3 products (many industrial electronics, components) carry 25%. List 4B consumer electronics (phones, laptops as of 2020) carry 7.5%. These rates add directly on top of the MFN rate.

3

IEEPA Executive Order Tariffs (2025–2026)

Imposed under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act starting in early 2025. These added additional layers on Chinese goods — initially up to 145% in April 2025, then reduced through trade negotiations. As of mid-2026, the effective IEEPA surcharge on Chinese electronics sits in the 30–55% range following the US-China trade framework agreed in May 2025, though specific rates depend on product category and ongoing negotiations.

Electronics from Non-China Countries: A Very Different Picture

Not all electronics face the same tariff pile-on. If your product comes from Vietnam, Taiwan, South Korea, Mexico, or most of Europe, you're looking at dramatically lower rates.

Under the WTO Information Technology Agreement, which the US and 82 other countries signed, most electronics products enter the US at 0% MFN duty. This covers computers, phones, semiconductors, telecom equipment, and hundreds of other product categories. Countries like Vietnam, Taiwan, and South Korea benefit from this agreement and face zero or near-zero tariffs on most electronics — no Section 301, no IEEPA surcharges.

Mexico adds another advantage: under USMCA, qualifying electronics with sufficient North American content enter at 0%. Many US importers have been shifting assembly operations to Mexico precisely to eliminate the China tariff exposure. See our USMCA tariff benefits guide for the qualification rules.

How to Calculate Your Actual Electronics Import Cost

The tariff is applied to the customs value of your shipment — not your selling price, not your landed cost. Customs value is typically the transaction value (what you paid the seller), including the cost of goods but generally excluding US domestic freight after entry.

Customs Value = FOB price paid to supplier

Tariff Due = Customs Value × Combined Tariff Rate

Example: $50,000 of TVs from China at ~70% effective rate
= $50,000 × 0.70 = $35,000 in duties

On top of the tariff, you'll also pay the Merchandise Processing Fee (MPF) — 0.3464% of customs value, with a minimum of $31.67 and a maximum of $614.35 per entry. And if your goods arrive by ocean, there may be Harbor Maintenance Fee (HMF) charges too. These fees are relatively small compared to the tariff itself but show up on every entry. The De Minimis Loophole — Now Closed for China

Until 2025, consumers and small importers used the Section 321 de minimis exemption to import electronics from China duty-free in packages under $800. That loophole is effectively closed. As of May 2025, the de minimis exemption no longer applies to goods of Chinese origin — every package, regardless of value, faces the full tariff stack.

This change hit platforms like Temu, Shein, and individual AliExpress buyers hard. A $60 phone case that previously arrived duty-free now technically faces the same percentage tariff as a $6,000 pallet. Read more in our de minimis China 2026 guide.

Strategies to Reduce Tariffs on Electronics

There are legitimate ways to reduce your tariff exposure. None of them are magic, and some require significant operational changes, but they work.

1. Source Diversification (China + 1)

The most common strategy. Move final assembly or sourcing to Vietnam, Taiwan, India, or Mexico. Apple has been doing this at scale since 2022. The tradeoff is higher unit costs in many cases, but the tariff savings often more than compensate at volume. Vietnam has become the dominant alternative for consumer electronics assembly — see our China Plus One strategy guide for the mechanics.

2. First-Sale Valuation

If your supply chain has multiple transactions (factory → middleman → you), you may be able to declare customs value based on the first-sale price (factory price) rather than the last sale (middleman price). This legally reduces the dutiable value. It requires documentation but can cut 10–20% off your tariff bill on complex supply chains. See our guide on reducing import tariffs for details.

3. Bonded Warehouses & FTZs

Foreign Trade Zones (FTZs) let you import goods without paying duties immediately. You only pay when the goods leave the FTZ for US commerce — or zero duty if you re-export. For electronics that may be re-exported or held in inventory pending trade deal changes, FTZs can provide cash flow relief and potential savings.

4. Section 301 Exclusion Requests

USTR has run several rounds of Section 301 exclusion reviews since 2018. Importers can petition for product-specific exclusions if they can demonstrate US supply alternatives don't exist. The process is competitive and not guaranteed, but approved exclusions have been worth millions in saved duties for some electronics importers. Check our Section 301 refund status guide for the latest.

What This Means for Electronics Sellers and Amazon FBA

If you sell electronics on Amazon — whether as a private label brand or a reseller — the tariff increase is a direct hit to your margin unless you've already repriced or switched suppliers. A product that cost you $15 landed from China two years ago may cost $20–25 today after tariffs, while your competition has likely repriced too.

The competitive landscape has shifted: sellers who moved to Vietnam or India suppliers in 2022–2024 are now operating at significantly lower COGS than those still buying from China. That gap is real and growing. Read our tariffs for Amazon FBA sellers guide for category-specific breakdowns and margin modeling.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are US tariffs on electronics from China permanent?

The Section 301 tariffs (7.5–25%) have been in place since 2018 and show no sign of being fully removed — both the Trump and Biden administrations maintained them. The IEEPA surcharges added in 2025 are executive actions that could be modified or removed through trade negotiations. As of mid-2026, the US-China framework agreement reduced some IEEPA rates but left Section 301 tariffs intact. Treat current rates as a floor, not a temporary anomaly.

Do tariffs apply to electronics I personally import?

Technically yes, but in practice, personal imports under $800 from non-China countries still qualify for de minimis (Section 321) exemption. For goods of Chinese origin, de minimis was eliminated in 2025 — every shipment faces duties regardless of value. If you order a phone directly from a Chinese seller, duties apply. Most major retailers absorb these costs into retail pricing rather than charging you separately at the border.

How do I find the exact HTS code for my electronics product?

Start with the USITC's online tariff database at hts.usitc.gov. Electronics generally fall in Chapters 84 (machinery, computers, printers) and 85 (electrical equipment, phones, TVs, batteries). For complex products, a licensed customs broker can classify your goods — misclassification is a common and costly mistake, since the difference between two similar HTS codes can be a 17.5% tariff rate swing. See our guide on calculating import duty for the classification process.

Can I get a refund on tariffs already paid?

If your goods were imported under an HTS code that later received a Section 301 exclusion, you can file for a refund within the allowed window. The duty drawback program also allows refunds when imported goods are re-exported. Check the current status of refund opportunities in our Section 301 refund guide and IEEPA refund guide.
