7 Ways to Legally Reduce Your US Import Tariffs in 2026

Updated April 2026 · 10 min read

With effective tariff rates exceeding 40% on many imported goods in 2026, finding legal ways to reduce your duty burden isn't optional — it's essential for survival. Here are seven proven strategies that importers use to lower their costs without breaking any rules.

Strategy #1

Optimize Your HTS Classification

The Harmonized Tariff Schedule has over 10,000 product classifications, and similar products can fall under codes with vastly different duty rates. A wireless bluetooth speaker might be classified under a heading with a 4.9% rate or under one with 0% — depending on its specific features and construction.

Work with a licensed customs broker to review your classifications. Many importers use the first code they find without exploring alternatives. A thorough classification review is the single highest-ROI activity in tariff reduction.

Potential savings: 5–20% on duty rates

Strategy #2

Use Foreign Trade Zones (FTZs)

Foreign Trade Zones are designated areas within the US where goods can be imported, stored, assembled, or manufactured without immediately paying duties. FTZs offer several powerful advantages.

You can defer duty payments until goods leave the zone and enter US commerce. If you re-export goods, you pay zero duties. For manufacturing operations, you can choose to pay duties on either the imported components or the finished product — whichever has the lower rate (known as "inverted tariff" savings).

There are over 190 FTZs across the United States. Many logistics companies operate within FTZs and can handle the administrative requirements for you. Shipping platforms like Easyship can also help you compare rates and calculate duties automatically.

Potential savings: Duty deferral + 5–25% on manufactured goods

Strategy #3

Leverage Free Trade Agreements

The US has free trade agreements with 20 countries. The most impactful for importers in 2026 is USMCA (US-Mexico-Canada Agreement), which exempts qualifying goods from the 15% Section 122 surcharge — a significant advantage over sourcing from other countries.

Other active FTAs include agreements with Australia, South Korea (KORUS), Singapore, Chile, Colombia, Peru, and several Central American and Caribbean nations (CAFTA-DR). Each agreement has specific rules of origin that products must meet to qualify for preferential rates.

Potential savings: 15% or more on qualifying goods

Strategy #4

Apply for Section 301 Exclusions

If you import from China, USTR periodically opens exclusion request windows for specific products subject to Section 301 tariffs. An approved exclusion removes the additional 7.5–25% Section 301 duty from your products.

The process requires demonstrating that the product is not available from non-Chinese sources and that the tariff causes severe economic harm. Applications are product-specific using HTS codes. While the approval rate varies, successful exclusions can save tens of thousands of dollars per year on recurring imports.

Potential savings: 7.5–25% on Chinese goods

Strategy #5

Review Your Customs Valuation

Duties are calculated on the "customs value" of goods, which includes the transaction price plus certain additions (like assists, royalties, and packing costs) minus allowable deductions. Many importers overpay because they include costs that should be excluded from customs value.

For example, buying commissions paid to your own purchasing agent are generally deductible. Post-importation costs like domestic freight, installation, and assembly should not be included. International freight and insurance calculations should use actual costs, not inflated estimates.

Potential savings: 2–10% on customs value

Strategy #6

Consider Duty Drawback

If you import goods and later export them (either in their original form or as part of a finished product), you can recover up to 99% of the duties you paid through the duty drawback program. This is particularly valuable for manufacturers who import components, assemble finished products, and export some of them.

The drawback process requires careful record-keeping and filing within specific timeframes, but the refunds can be substantial. Many customs brokers specialize in drawback claims and work on a contingency basis — they only get paid when you receive your refund.

Potential savings: Up to 99% refund on re-exported goods

Strategy #7

Diversify Your Supply Chain

With Chinese goods facing combined tariff rates of 40–130%, sourcing from alternative countries can dramatically reduce your duty burden. Vietnam, India, Thailand, and Indonesia offer competitive manufacturing costs with significantly lower tariff exposure.

While these countries face the 15% Section 122 surcharge, they avoid the 7.5–100% Section 301 tariffs that apply exclusively to Chinese goods. The net savings can be 20–80% on duties alone, even before considering any differences in product costs.

Potential savings: 20–80% vs. China sourcing

Getting Started

The biggest mistake importers make is accepting their current duty costs as fixed. Every one of these strategies is legal, proven, and used by businesses of all sizes. Start with the easiest wins — HTS classification review and customs valuation — and work toward more complex strategies like FTZs and supply chain diversification as your import volume grows.
